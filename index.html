What is a Nebula?

A nebula is a giant cloud of dust and gas made from the remnants of a dead star. When a star is alive, it spends the majority of its time doing a process called nuclear fusion. Fusion is kind of like adding two elements together to make one bigger element instead. Stars like to use the smallest elements first, meaning it always starts fusing hydrogen into helium, but in order to pull off this process, it needs a lot of hydrogen. Eventually, it runs out of hydrogen and starts using helium instead. It can continue fusion all the way until it reaches iron, at which point, there's no way for it to make any more energy, and it dies. When it reaches the end of its lifespan, the star explodes, flinging all the elements it created into a giant cloud in space. We call these clouds “nebula.” The bigger the star, the more dust is ejected in the explosion, and the bigger the nebula.

The dust and gas are ejected from the dead star with such force that it slams together and starts spinning. Eventually, it starts to attract more and more dust from the cloud and starts to get bigger. When it gets enough material, it starts nuclear fusion, making it a brand-new star! In this sense, nebulas can be thought of as both a graveyard and a nursery, since they're a product of a dead star, but also responsible for creating new ones. That means that nothing is ever actually destroyed in the universe. It's just turned into something new!


How Are Nebulas Categorized?

In total, there are 5 categories for nebulas. There are Emission Nebulas, Reflection Nebulas, and Absorption (or dark) Nebulas, which are all defined by the appearance of light, and there are also Planetary Nebulas, and Supernova Remnants, which are defined by the size of the star that died in order to create the nebula. A nebula can actually fit into several of these categories at once.
