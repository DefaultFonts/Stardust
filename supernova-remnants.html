SUPERNOVA REMNANTS

What is a Supernova Remnant?

When a high-mass star dies, it does so with such a high amount of energy and force that any dust or gas around it collides in a powerful shock wave. Whereas a low-mass star dies in waves (ejecting the outermost layers one by one), a high-mass star ejects the majority of its' mass all at once in a massive explosion. When the explosion occurs, the gas itself can become so hot that the glow it produces outshines even our own sun, but this glow only lasts a short time (a couple of weeks or months) before dying down again. After a high-mass star ejects the majority of its mass, it then collapses on itself, creating either a black hole or a pulsar.


Why do we call it a Supernova Remnant?

These nebulas are literally just the remains of a supernova explosion. It includes all the gas and dust that were ejected by the star, as well as any other gas or dust that it may have caught in the path of the explosion.
