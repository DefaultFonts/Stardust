EMISSION NEBULAS

What is an Emission Nebula?

This type of nebula produces its own light thanks to the stars that are forming in the middle of the cloud. The newborn stars produce a lot of heat, and that heat causes the cloud of dust and gas to glow. Only stars hotter than 25,000 K are able to do this, however. Any star colder than 25,000 K will create a Reflection Nebula instead. Oftentimes, emission nebulas look red, since there is an abundance of hydrogen present, which glows red when it is heated up. Since many stars are forming from the same cloud of dust and gas, their light allows for the majority of the cloud to glow and be seen, rather than just one section of the cloud.


Why do we call it an Emission Nebula?

The gas and dust in the cloud of an emission nebula become so hot that it begins to emit its own light. Usually, an object in space that isn't a star can only be seen because it reflects light from a nearby star. In other words, we can only see certain objects because light shines on them from a nearby star. In the case of emission nebulas, they actually produce their own light.
